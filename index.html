For this assignment, we chose to analyze the Gentrification dataset.

Black Hat Write-Up:

For the black hat, the visualization is misleading in these three categories: marks/encodings, data transformation, titles/labels.


For encodings, the black hat color-coded the data as green for gentrified and red for non-gentrified to convey the idea that gentrification is good.


For data transformation, the black hat uses only data from Maryland because cherrypicking Maryland supports the claim that gentrification is correlated with positive home value/growth, education, and income; if other regions were considered in the data, it would have conflicted with the idea that gentrification has benefits to quality of life. Additionally, the y-axis is scaled by setting the max range to match the value for the “True” bar on each graph. This was done so that the gentrification would look more desirable being taller and complete if the bar reaches the max for each graph.


For titles/labels, the title is labeled as “The Effect of Gentrification in the United States” to mislead readers into thinking the visualization is unbiased and generalizable, when in reality, the data was cherrypicked from Maryland. The subtext also includes something along the lines of “associated with the University of Maryland” to add some deceiving credibility. Also, the y-axis is ambiguously labeled, so the reader cannot really tell what the metric is or how it’s aggregated (i.e., amount in dollars, percent, etc and average, sum, median, etc).


As for the visualization design, the black hat is cleanly presented to appear professional, with the attribution to University of Maryland to deceive the reader into thinking the black hat is even more credible. The black hat has several categories to compare so that the evidence appears not only more compelling but also visually attractive.


White Hat Write-Up:

For the white hat, the visualization was made as a direct counter to the black hat, so that the data can directly show how the black hat is a sham by having good marks/encodings, data transformation, and titles & labels.


For marks/encodings, the white hat keeps the gentrified color-coding with false-gentrification as red and true-gentrification as green, the reason being that it would be confusing to flip the colors because the white hat is attempting to counter the black ha, so it matches the color-code to directly compare the height of the bars from each hat.


For data transformations, the white hat not only also looks into the same home value, education, and income data as does the black hat, the white hat also filters the data by clearly showing where the data is coming from: cities. The cities give the reader even more information and trust about where the gentrification data is coming from, contrasting to the black hat, which only cherrypicks data from Maryland but then claims the data is generalized from the entire United States.


For titles & labels, in order to further contrast the black hat, the white hat copies the title but then italicizes the word “actually” to show how this white hat actually and accurately represents the data. The white hat also keeps the subtitle so that readers can still be reassured of this white hat’s credibility. Furthermore, the y-axis labels are accurately labeled with the unit so that the reader can know the exact values of the bars instead of being ambiguously confuzzled with the black hat’s improperly labeled y-axis.
